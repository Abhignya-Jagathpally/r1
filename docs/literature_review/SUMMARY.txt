MAJOR CONTRADICTIONS IDENTIFIED: 10
Most significant:
1. TabPFN superiority: Wins on benchmarks (Nature 2024), loses on clinical data (medRxiv 2026)
2. AI complexity: Complex models marginally better (0.05 AUROC) than simple (200x cost premium)
3. Maturity gap: Academic progress rapid, clinical translation slower than publications suggest
4. RWE paradigm: Shifting from "generalization test" to "distinct evidence source" requiring separate models

FOUNDATIONAL CONCEPTS ANALYZED: 3
1. International Staging System (ISS) → evolution through R-ISS to R2-ISS (2005-2022)
   Unresolved: Genomic complexity vs. simplicity tradeoff
2. Measurable Residual Disease (MRD) → from prognostic marker to treatment-guided (2004-2024)
   Unresolved: MRD independence vs. confounding by therapy type
3. Machine Learning → from univariate to multi-task deep learning (1990s-2024)
   Key finding: Tree-based ensembles dominate, not transformers

RESEARCH GAPS IDENTIFIED: 5 major unanswered questions
1. Data modality integration (genomics vs labs vs MRD) - Timeline: 18-24 mo, Cost: $500K-1M
2. Treatment heterogeneity in generalization (how do models degrade over time) - 24-36 mo, $800K-1.2M
3. MRD-guided escalation algorithm (when/how to intensify) - 18-24 mo, $400K-600K
4. Prospective clinical validation RCT (does AI improve outcomes) - 36-48 mo, $2M-3.5M [CRITICAL GAP]
5. Digital twin treatment simulation (personalized treatment selection) - 36-48 mo, $1.5M-2.5M

METHODOLOGY ANALYSIS:
Dominant: Retrospective model development + external validation (41% of papers)
Underused: Prospective RCTs (0% for AI prediction), cost-effectiveness (7%), implementation science (7%)
Critical gap: Zero RCTs validating AI predictor in MM - this blocks clinical translation
Consensus emerging: Gradient boosting on tabular data outperforms deep learning and TabPFN

WHAT TO BUILD (Model Strategy):
1. Start with gradient boosting on routine labs + R2-ISS clinical features
   - Expected performance: AUROC 0.85-0.87 (matches benchmark)
   - Cost: Low (~$50-100K for development)
   - Interpretability: High (SHAP for explanations)

2. Add genomics sequentially:
   - Baseline: Genomic subtype classification for treatment intensity selection
   - Marginal gain: ~0.05 AUROC improvement over clinical staging alone
   - Cost: ~$2,000-5,000 per patient (50x labs cost)
   - Decision: Only include if external validation shows meaningful improvement

3. Incorporate MRD dynamically:
   - Progression tracking: Serial MRD measurements + routine labs
   - Use case: Predict relapse risk post-treatment, guide escalation timing
   - Temporal model: Gradient boosting or temporal fusion transformer for time series

WHAT NOT TO BUILD (Yet):
- TabPFN: Promising on benchmarks, not proven superior on clinical MM data
- Complex transformers: Not necessary for tabular MM data; tree-based methods sufficient
- Overly complex genomic models: 50-90 genes show minimal benefit; start with R2-ISS factors

WHEN TO VALIDATE PROSPECTIVELY:
- After retrospective external validation on 2+ independent cohorts (AUROC 0.87+)
- Plan prospective real-world cohort (12-24 months) to assess real-world performance
- Then RCT (36-48 months) to prove outcome benefit vs. standard care
- Don't skip this step: retrospective validation insufficient for clinical adoption

- Answer: Labs sufficient for progression tracking; genomics for baseline stratification (complementary)

================================================================================
CRITICAL GAPS BLOCKING TRANSLATION
================================================================================

1. NO PROSPECTIVE RCT OF AI PREDICTOR IN MM
   Gap: Retrospective models excellent (AUROC 0.87), but unknown if implementation improves outcomes
   Solution: Design & fund multi-center RCT (primary recommendation)
   Impact: Required for clinical adoption, FDA approval, health system deployment

2. MINIMAL COST-EFFECTIVENESS ANALYSIS
   Gap: No studies comparing genomics ($2K) vs. labs ($100) for outcomes achieved
   Solution: Include health economics alongside model development
   Impact: Informs resource allocation decisions; justifies model complexity

3. LIMITED IMPLEMENTATION SCIENCE
   Gap: Unknown if clinicians will trust and use AI predictions
   Solution: Parallel user research; workflow optimization; clinician perception studies
   Impact: Prevents "shelf-ware" — models not adopted despite good performance

4. TREATMENT HETEROGENEITY NOT ADDRESSED
   Gap: Models trained on 2009-2019 data; treatment landscape evolved (CAR-T, bispecific)
   Solution: Plan continuous model updates; track temporal generalization
   Impact: Models degrade over time; require retraining as treatments change

5. DATA MODALITY COMBINATION UNKNOWN
   Gap: Labs (AUROC 0.87) vs. genomics (c-index 0.78) never directly compared on same cohort
   Solution: Prospective study comparing genomics + labs combination
   Impact: Unknown if 100x cost of genomics justified by accuracy gains

EVIDENCE HIERARCHY FOR YOUR PROGRAM
================================================================================

1. STRONGEST: Prospective RCT of AI intervention (if positive)
   - Proves outcome improvement in real-world setting
   - Enables clinical adoption & FDA approval
   - Current status: 0 in MM AI literature [OPPORTUNITY]

2. STRONG: Prospective real-world cohort (outcomes tracked with AI in use)
   - Demonstrates real-world accuracy & clinician adoption
   - Identifies implementation barriers
   - Can start after retrospective validation

3. STRONG: Multiple external validation (2+ independent cohorts)
   - Demonstrates generalization across populations
   - Sufficient for academic credibility
   - Current status: Most modern papers do this

4. MODERATE: Single external validation
   - Standard for ML papers; good but incomplete
   - Risk: Model may not generalize to 3rd cohort

5. LOW: Internal validation only
   - High overfitting risk; limited generalization evidence
   - Acceptable for early research, not for clinical use

6. MINIMAL: Cost-effectiveness analysis
   - Few papers include this; critical for real-world decisions
   - Often missing from AI literature
