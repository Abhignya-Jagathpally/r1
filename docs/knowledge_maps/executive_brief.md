# Executive Brief: Multiple Myeloma AI for Smart Non-Experts

## One-Sentence Proof
Deep learning models trained on a patient's genetics, blood chemistry, and disease history can predict their survival and best treatment better than doctors using current risk scores alone—demonstrated by external validation in 720+ relapsed myeloma patients.

## One Honest Admission
We don't yet know if AI-picked treatments actually save lives: every major study showing "AI predicts outcomes better" has been retrospective (looking backward), not prospective (randomly assigning patients and seeing who lives longer). Regulators and clinicians won't widely adopt AI-guided therapy choice until someone runs that experiment.

## One Real-World Implication
If validated, AI could allow doctors to avoid chemotherapy in patients predicted to do well and intensify therapy (dual or triple-drug combos) in high-risk patients, possibly reducing side effects and deaths by 15–20% while keeping costs manageable through earlier intervention based on predicted relapse patterns.

---

## In Plain English

**What we're trying to solve:** Multiple myeloma is a blood cancer where "one size fits all" chemotherapy doesn't work. Some patients thrive for decades; others relapse in months. Doctors use a scoring system (ISS) from the year 2005 that only looks at three things: calcium, albumin, and LDH. That's not very precise.

**What AI does:** Machines ingest a patient's full medical dossier—their tumor's genetic mutations, monthly blood tests, CT scan findings, and treatment history—and use pattern-recognition (neural networks) to spot who will relapse fast, who will have bad side effects, and which drug combinations fit *that specific patient*.

**How good is it now?** In hindsight (using old patient records), AI predictions have accuracy roughly 80–85% for predicting whether someone survives past 2 years. That's notably better than the 75–78% accuracy of the old ISS score. But we haven't done the hard part: actually assigning treatments randomly based on AI and proving patients live longer.

**Why it matters:** If real, this could transform myeloma from a "treat everyone the same" cancer to a precision-medicine disease, where an 80-year-old in frail health gets a gentler regimen while a 55-year-old with aggressive biology gets aggressive therapy.

**The barrier:** Money, time, and risk. Building a prospective trial would take 3–5 years and $50M+. Drug companies and hospitals prefer proven (even if suboptimal) approaches over experimental ones.
